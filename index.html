Finish Selection Guide

Match surface targets to forming, cosmetic expectations, and corrosion protection before freezing the process.

Mechanical Finishes

  • Brushed / Directional: Uniform grain hides handling marks; ideal for exposed stainless panels and consumer enclosures.
  • Vibratory / Random: Abrasive media tumbles parts for an even sheen—great for deburring before coating.
  • Grinding: Tight tolerance surfaces or weld blend-out before painting; fine wheels reach Ra 16-32 µin.

Chemical & Electrochemical

  • Pickling & Passivation: Removes scale and restores corrosion resistance after welding or hot work.
  • Anodizing: Controlled oxide on aluminum for corrosion resistance and color options.
  • Electropolish: Dissolves surface peaks for Ra < 8 µin; common in sanitary and semiconductor hardware.

Protective Coatings

  • Powder Coat: Durable thermoset film that hides minor imperfections; aim for Ra <= 125 µin before coating.
  • Zinc Plating / Galvanizing: Sacrificial corrosion protection for steel. Hot-dip galvanizing leaves a heavy spangle (~200 µin).
  • Conversion Coating: Chromate or phosphate pretreatments boost paint adhesion and corrosion resistance.

Use the right standards, symbols, and inspection notes to prevent rework.

Drawing Tips

  • Use the Ra symbol (e.g., Ra 63) near critical surfaces and call out units.
  • Specify grain direction for brushed finishes relative to edges or features.
  • Include post-weld finishing requirements (grind flush, blend to match No. 4 grain) to align expectations.

Design Considerations

Balance welding, forming, and corrosion concerns when specifying alloys.

Welding & Heat Input

  • Match filler metals to base chemistry and strength; use low-hydrogen processes on high-strength steels.
  • Heat-treated aluminums (6061-T6, 7075) soften in the HAZ—plan for post-weld heat treat or design around it.
  • Stainless retains heat, so plan for distortion control and post-weld cleaning.

Forming Allowances

  • HSLA steels often need ≥2T bend radii to prevent cracking—check mill bend tables.
  • Run test bends when switching aluminum tempers; 5052-H32 outperforms 6061-T6 in tight bends.
  • Form precipitation-hardening stainless in the solution-treated condition before aging.

Corrosion & Coatings

  • Painting carbon steel? Target Ra <= 125 µin and a clean, dry surface for adhesion.
  • Isolate aluminum from stainless in wet environments to avoid galvanic corrosion.
  • Passivate or pickle stainless after fabrication to restore corrosion resistance.

Weld Symbol Reference

Interpret common AWS/AISC welding symbols directly from fabrication drawings.

Quick sketches and callouts for frequently specified weld symbols used in shop drawings and field fit-up notes.

Fillet Weld

Triangle symbol indicates a fillet weld. Leg size is shown to the left of the symbol and length to the right.

Square Groove

Parallel plate edges receive a square groove weld. Groove depth or effective throat appears to the left of the symbol.

V-Groove

Beveled members form a V-groove. Included angle and root opening are specified with the symbol.

Plug / Slot

Circular or elongated welds joining overlapping plates. Symbol shows hole diameter and spacing.

Spot Weld

Resistance or spot welds are shown with a circle on the reference line. Diameter and pitch appear alongside.

Field Weld

Flag at the reference line intersection calls for welding to be performed in the field rather than in shop fabrication.

Bevel Groove

Single-bevel groove welds show a sloped member on one side of the joint. Symbol designates groove depth and root opening.

J-Groove

J-groove welds combine a straight edge and a radius. Use when only one member can be prepared with a bevel.

Flare Bevel

Used when round or curved members meet a flat surface. Required weld size is listed to the left of the symbol.

Seam Weld

Parallel lines on the reference line indicate a seam or continuous resistance weld. Pitch and length appear with the symbol.

Weld All Around

A circle at the reference line intersection means the weld continues completely around the joint.

Arrow Side / Other Side
Symbols placed below the reference line apply to the arrow side of the joint; symbols above apply to the opposite side.
Tail Callouts
The tail is used for process notes such as SMAW, GMAW, or backing requirements. Omit the tail when no notes are needed.
Finish Symbols
Letters such as G, M, or C indicate grinding, machining, or chipping of the finished weld surface.

GD&T Symbol Reference

Common geometric characteristic symbols for ASME Y14.5 feature control frames.

Use these quick sketches to identify form, orientation, location, and runout symbols when reading drawings or programming inspection.

Straightness

Controls how much a surface or axis may deviate from a perfect straight line along its length.

Flatness

Limits the allowable out-of-plane deviation for a surface without reference to a datum.

Circularity

Controls roundness of a surface cross-section. Measured independent of a datum.

Cylindricity

Combines circularity and straightness to limit how much a cylindrical surface may vary.

Perpendicularity

Ensures a surface or axis is 90° to a datum plane or axis within the specified tolerance zone.

Parallelism

Controls how evenly separated a surface or axis must be from a datum plane.

Position

Defines the allowable deviation of a feature's location relative to datums; core for true position callouts.

Concentricity

Keeps median points of a feature common to a datum axis; compares centerlines rather than surfaces.

Symmetry

Controls how evenly a feature is balanced about a datum plane; evaluates median points on opposing surfaces.

Circular Runout

Limits variation of a surface as it is rotated 360° about a datum axis; checks circular elements individually.

Total Runout

Controls surface variation across the entire rotation and length of a feature relative to a datum axis.

Feature Control Frame
GD&T symbols live inside a frame with tolerance values and datum references. Read from left to right.
Material Condition Modifiers
Symbols such as Ⓜ (MMC), Ⓛ (LMC), and Ⓢ (RFS) modify tolerance zones based on feature size.
Datum Feature Symbols
Datums establish the coordinate system; ensure datum callouts on drawings match inspection setup.

Tolerances by Fit Class

Compare typical clearance, transition, and interference fits using ISO tolerance zones and practical ranges.

Match hole and shaft tolerance designations to achieve the required running clearance or interference for rotating components and press fits.

Clearance Fits (Sliding & Running)

Maintain positive space between mating parts for free movement, lubrication, or easy assembly.

Loose Running — H11/c11

Clearance: 0.25-0.60 mm

Low-precision sliding where contamination is likely.

Free Running — H9/d9

Clearance: 0.13-0.36 mm

General-purpose machine fits; hand assembly easy.

Close Running — H8/f7

Clearance: 0.02-0.09 mm

Guided shafts or slides needing consistent motion.

Sliding Fit — H7/g6

Clearance: 0.01-0.04 mm

Tooling fixtures and precision ways with lubrication.

Transition Fits (Line-to-Light Interference)

Allow slight clearance or interference depending on actual sizes, providing accurate alignment with manageable assembly force.

Line Fit — H7/k6

Range: -0.02 to +0.02 mm

Alignment dowels, slip fit at smaller diameters.

Sliding Transition — H7/j6

Range: -0.01 to +0.03 mm

Gear hubs and couplings needing accurate concentricity.

Snug Transition — H7/m6

Range: 0 to +0.03 mm

Armatures, bearings, and assemblies requiring precise location.

Interference Fits (Press & Shrink)

Ensure negative clearance so parts must be pressed or heated for assembly, creating a rigid joint.

Light Press — H7/p6

Interference: 0.01-0.05 mm

Pulleys, medium torque bushings; hand press or mild heat.

Medium Press — H7/r6

Interference: 0.03-0.08 mm

Heavy-duty hubs or rigid couplings requiring arbor press.

Shrink Fit — H7/u6

Interference: 0.06-0.14 mm

High-torque gears and critical rotating members; heat assembly.

Heavy Interference — H7/x6

Interference: 0.09-0.20 mm

Permanent structural joints; heat differential or hydraulic press.

ISO Fit Notation
Capital letters denote hole tolerance zones, lowercase for shafts (e.g., H7/g6). Combine to target desired clearance.
Production Notes
Transition and interference fits often require temperature control or press-force calculations—note assembly method on drawings.
Surface Finish
Consistent finish improves repeatability. Check bearings, seals, and lubrication when choosing a fit class.
